What is "Negligence"? 

Negligence is a legal concept used to determine liability when one person’s failure to exercise reasonable care results in harm to another. It is the core of most personal injury and property damage claims.

To establish negligence, four specific elements must generally be proven:

Duty of Care: The individual had a legal obligation to act with reasonable caution toward others (e.g., a driver has a duty to follow traffic laws to protect other motorists).

Breach of Duty: The individual failed to meet that standard of care through an action or a failure to act (e.g., running a red light).

Causation: The breach of duty directly caused the injury or damage. It must be shown that the harm would not have occurred "but for" the individual's actions.

Damages: The victim suffered actual losses, such as physical injury, emotional distress, or financial costs (e.g., medical bills or car repair costs).

Standard of Care
The law typically measures conduct against the "reasonable person" standard. This asks whether a person of ordinary prudence would have acted differently under the same circumstances.

Types of Negligence
Laws regarding negligence vary significantly by jurisdiction, particularly concerning how fault is shared:

Comparative Negligence: Damages are reduced based on the percentage of fault attributed to the claimant.

Contributory Negligence: In some regions, if the claimant is found even slightly responsible for the accident, they may be barred from recovering any damages.

Gross Negligence: A more severe form of negligence involving a conscious and voluntary disregard of the need to use reasonable care, which is likely to cause foreseeable grave injury or harm.

Practical Next Steps
Document Evidence: Collect photos, witness contact information, and reports (such as police or incident reports) that show how an incident occurred.

Track Damages: Keep detailed records of all financial losses, including medical receipts, lost wages, and repair estimates.

Verify Local Laws: Consult the specific statutes or legal standards in your jurisdiction, as the rules for proving negligence and the timelines for filing a claim (statute of limitations) differ by region.

A wrongful death claim is a civil action brought when a person dies due to the legal fault of another person or entity. Filing procedures and eligibility vary significantly by jurisdiction, but generally follow a specific legal framework.

Legal Standing: Who Can Be the Lead Plaintiff
"Standing" refers to the legal right to file the lawsuit. This is strictly defined by state or regional statutes. Generally, those with standing fall into these categories:

Immediate Family Members: In all jurisdictions, spouses and children (including adopted children) of the deceased have standing. Parents of unmarried minor children also typically have standing.

Financial Dependents: Some regions allow domestic partners, putative spouses, or anyone who was financially dependent on the deceased to file.

Distant Family Members: Some jurisdictions allow siblings or grandparents to file if no immediate family members are alive.

The Personal Representative: In many jurisdictions, the claim must be filed by the executor or administrator of the deceased person’s estate on behalf of the surviving beneficiaries.

Step-by-Step Filing Process
Identify the Personal Representative: Determine if the court must first appoint an executor or administrator for the estate. This individual often acts as the formal plaintiff.

Establish Proof of Negligence: You must be able to prove the same four elements required in a standard negligence case: duty, breach, causation, and damages.

Calculate Damages: Wrongful death damages typically include:

Medical and funeral expenses.

Loss of the deceased person's expected income.

Loss of companionship, guidance, and consortium.

Pain and suffering experienced by the survivors.

Verify the Statute of Limitations: Every jurisdiction has a strict deadline for filing a wrongful death claim, often ranging from one to three years from the date of death. Missing this deadline usually results in a permanent loss of the right to sue.

File the Complaint: A formal legal document (the "Complaint") must be drafted and filed in the appropriate civil court, detailing the facts of the case and the legal basis for the claim.

Service of Process: The defendant (the party responsible for the death) must be formally notified of the lawsuit according to legal service rules.

Key Considerations
Criminal vs. Civil: A wrongful death claim is a civil action. It is separate from criminal charges (like manslaughter). A defendant can be sued for wrongful death even if they are acquitted of criminal charges, as the "preponderance of evidence" standard in civil court is lower than "beyond a reasonable doubt" in criminal court.

Immunity Issues: Some government entities or employees may have "sovereign immunity," which can limit or prevent certain wrongful death claims unless specific waivers apply.

Survival Actions: Some jurisdictions distinguish between a wrongful death claim (for the survivors' losses) and a "survival action," which seeks damages for the pain and suffering the deceased experienced prior to death.

Should you sue a "judgment-proof" individual (no assets) for an accident, or focus on a "Third-Party" claim against an insured entity? 

Deciding whether to pursue a judgment-proof individual or a third-party entity depends on the likelihood of actual recovery versus the cost of litigation.

Suing a "Judgment-Proof" Individual
An individual is "judgment-proof" if they lack insurance, significant income, or seizable assets (like real estate or investments).

The Risk: Winning a lawsuit results in a judgment, which is a piece of paper stating the person owes you money. If the individual has no assets, you cannot collect.

The Cost: You may spend thousands in legal fees and court costs to obtain a judgment that remains unpaid.

Exceptions: In some jurisdictions, judgments remain valid for many years (e.g., 10–20 years) and can be renewed. If the individual inherits money, wins the lottery, or secures a high-paying job later, you may be able to collect then via wage garnishment or bank levies.

Focusing on a Third-Party Claim
A third-party claim targets an entity or individual other than the direct person involved who may share legal responsibility.

The Advantage: Insured entities (corporations, employers, or property owners) have "deep pockets," meaning they have the financial resources or insurance coverage to pay a settlement or judgment.

Common Scenarios:

Vicarious Liability: If the individual was driving a company vehicle or working at the time of the accident, their employer may be liable.

Premises Liability: If a hazardous condition on a property contributed to the accident, the property owner or management company may be responsible.

Product Liability: If a mechanical failure (like brake failure) caused the accident, the manufacturer could be a target.

Social Host/Dram Shop: If the individual was over-served alcohol at a business, the establishment might be liable in some jurisdictions.

Strategic Next Steps
Asset Search: Conduct a basic asset check (public records search) to confirm if the individual truly has no attachable assets or insurance coverage.

Investigate Causation: Look for contributing factors. Was there a poorly maintained road, a faulty car part, or an employer involved? Identifying a third party is often the only way to ensure a financial recovery.

Check Your Own Insurance: If the individual is judgment-proof and no third party exists, you may need to file an Uninsured/Underinsured Motorist (UM/UIM) claim through your own insurance policy if the accident involved a vehicle.

Evaluate Cost-Benefit: If the only liable party is judgment-proof, consider whether the potential for future collection justifies the immediate legal expenses. Many attorneys will not take these cases on a contingency fee basis because the risk of non-payment is too high.
