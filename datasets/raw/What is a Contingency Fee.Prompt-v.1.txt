Contingency Fee

A contingency fee is a payment arrangement where your attorney only gets paid if you win your case or reach a settlement.

How it works:

    You pay nothing upfront
    If you win, the attorney takes an agreed percentage of your recovery
    If you lose, you owe the attorney no legal fees

Typical percentage:

    Usually 33% (one-third) of the settlement or award
    Can range from 25%–40% depending on the case complexity and whether it goes to trial

What it doesn't cover: Contingency fees cover attorney time only. You may still owe case expenses (court filing fees, expert witnesses, medical records) regardless of outcome — sometimes deducted from your settlement, sometimes billed separately. Clarify this in writing before signing.

Example:

    You settle for $90,000. At 33%, your attorney receives $30,000. You receive $60,000 — minus any case expenses.

Key consumer tips:

    Always get the fee agreement in writing
    Ask specifically: Are expenses included in the percentage, or deducted separately?
    Contingency fees are negotiable
    This arrangement is common in personal injury, slip and fall, and medical malpractice cases

    ⚠️ Fee structures and regulations vary by state. An attorney is required to explain all fees before you sign a retainer agreement.

Common uses:

    Personal injury cases (car accidents, slips and falls, medical malpractice)

    Some employment disputes, consumer protection, or class actions

MMI is the point at which a medical professional determines your condition has stabilized and no further significant improvement is expected, even with additional treatment.  

The Risks of a Quick Settlement
Finality: Once you sign a release and accept a settlement, you typically waive the right to seek further compensation. If new symptoms appear or a surgery is required later, the insurer is no longer liable.  

Undervaluation: Initial offers often cover immediate "liquidated" costs (current medical bills and lost wages) but rarely account for the full scope of future needs.  

Why Maximum Medical Improvement Matters
Reaching MMI allows you to accurately calculate the total "value" of the claim, which includes:

Future Medical Expenses: Permanent injuries may require ongoing physical therapy, medications, or prosthetic replacements.  

Permanent Disability/Impairment: If you do not return to 100% health, you may be eligible for compensation for "permanent partial disability."  

Loss of Earning Capacity: If the injury prevents you from returning to your previous profession, the long-term financial impact can only be assessed once your permanent limitations are known.

Step-by-Step Guidance
Prioritize Treatment: Follow your doctor’s prescribed plan. Do not rush to settle while you are still undergoing diagnostic testing or major procedures.

Request an MMI Evaluation: Once your recovery plateaus, your physician will issue a report stating you have reached MMI and may assign a "Permanent Impairment Rating."  

Document Residual Effects: Keep a log of activities you can no longer perform or pain levels that persist after reaching MMI.

Review the Statute of Limitations: While you should wait for MMI, you must be aware of the legal deadline to file a lawsuit in your state or province. If MMI takes years to reach, you may need to file a protective lawsuit before the deadline passes.

A faster settlement might be considered only if:

The injuries were demonstrably minor and fully healed (e.g., simple bruising or small cuts).

The insurance policy limits are very low and the initial offer already exhausts the total amount available.

It’s generally safer to wait until you reach Maximum Medical Improvement (MMI) before accepting a settlement, because settling early can permanently cut off compensation for future medical needs. 
Why waiting for MMI is usually the better choice

Across multiple legal and consumer‑protection sources, the guidance is consistent: settling before you finish treatment or reach MMI is risky.

Key reasons:

    A settlement is final. Once you accept a payout and sign a release, you cannot reopen the claim—even if you later discover you need surgery or long‑term care. 

    Your true medical costs aren’t known early on. Many injuries evolve over weeks or months. Some conditions (e.g., soft‑tissue injuries, nerve issues, post‑concussion symptoms) may not fully appear until later. 

    MMI gives a clearer picture of long‑term needs. Doctors can determine whether you have permanent limitations, need ongoing treatment, or face future medical expenses only after your condition stabilizes. 

    You negotiate from a stronger position. Complete medical records and a defined prognosis typically lead to more accurate—and often higher—settlement valuations. 

    Insurance companies push early settlements to minimize payouts. Quick offers (“swoop and settle”) often undervalue long‑term costs and rely on the fact that many people are under financial pressure. 

When might someone consider settling early?

While waiting is usually recommended, early settlement may be considered if:

    Injuries are clearly minor and fully resolved.

    You urgently need funds and understand the risks.

    A lawyer advises that the offer is fair given the circumstances.

Even then, the risk remains: you cannot claim additional compensation later if new symptoms or complications arise. 
Practical guidance

    Do not rely on the insurance adjuster’s opinion about when you’ve reached MMI—only your treating medical professionals can determine that. 

    Document all treatment, follow medical advice, and keep records of symptoms, limitations, and expenses.

    Consult a qualified personal injury attorney before accepting any settlement, especially if injuries are moderate or severe.
